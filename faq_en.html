FAQ

  • Q1: Why do plastic products sometimes show dye migration to the surface after dyeing?

    Reason: The migration of solvent dyes typically stems from their insufficient solubility in the plastic matrix or a poor affinity with the matrix, leading dye molecules to gradually migrate to the surface over time.

    Solution:

    • Select solvent dyes with high affinity and compatible solubility with the plastic substrate (e.g., Solvent Red 135 for PMMA).
    • Control the dye concentration (recommended range: 0.1%-2%) to avoid exceeding the substrate's saturation solubility.
    • Add anti-migration agents (such as low molecular weight polymers) to enhance the dye's stability within the substrate.
  • Q2: How can the lightfastness of dyed plastics be improved to prevent fading?

    Reason: Insufficient dye lightfastness allows UV irradiation to damage the dye's molecular structure, which is more pronounced in ABS or PMMA products used outdoors.

    Solution:

    • Choose solvent dyes with high lightfastness (e.g., anthraquinone-based Solvent Blue 104, lightfastness rating of 6-7).
    • Incorporate UV absorbers (such as benzotriazoles) or hindered amine light stabilizers (HALS) into the formulation.
    • Enhance the UV protection layer on the product surface to reduce direct UV exposure.
  • Q3: Why do solvent dyes sometimes dissolve incompletely in plastic films, resulting in color streaks?

    Reason: Low dye solubility or incompatibility with the plastic substrate may be due to the selection of an inappropriate dye type or uneven mixing.

    Solution:

    • Choose solvent dyes with good compatibility with the substrate (e.g., Solvent Yellow 163 for PC and PMMA).
    • Use a premixing technique: first dissolve the dye in a small amount of a compatible solvent (such as xylene), and then mix it with plastic pellets.
    • Utilize high-speed mixing equipment to ensure uniform dye dispersion.
  • Q4: How can I determine whether a dye's migration will affect product quality?

    Reason: Highly migratory dyes may cause color transfer upon contact with other materials or precipitate out under high-temperature, high-humidity conditions.

    Solution:

    • Conduct migration tests before use (e.g., press a dyed sample against white cloth at 60°C and 95% humidity for 24 hours and observe the results).
    • Select low-migration dyes (such as anthraquinone dyes) and avoid using volatile solvent carriers.
    • Increase the processing temperature (e.g., process PC at 280°C) to promote the integration of the dye with the substrate.
  • Q5: Can dyes with poor lightfastness not be used at all for outdoor products?

    Reason: Dyes with low lightfastness (e.g., certain azo dyes, lightfastness ratings of only 3-4) fade rapidly under prolonged outdoor exposure.

    Solution:

    • Select dyes with higher lightfastness ratings according to requirements (e.g., single or complex dyes with lightfastness ratings of 6 or higher).
    • If dyes with poor lightfastness must be used, combine them with UV absorbers and restrict usage scenarios (e.g., short-term displays).
    • Test color stability under outdoor exposure conditions (e.g., according to ISO 4892 standards) to confirm compliance with requirements.
  • Q6: What problems can arise from insufficient solvent dye solubility?

    Reason: Insufficient solubility can cause dye crystals to precipitate out, affecting the product's appearance (e.g., PMMA light boxes) or surface gloss.

    Solution:

    • Consult the dye's technical data to select products with high solubility (e.g., Solvent Green 3 has good solubility in PC).
    • Adjust the dye concentration to avoid exceeding the substrate's solubility limit (typically not exceeding 2%).
    • Add a solvent aid (such as a small amount of ester solvent) to the mixture to improve dye solubility.
  • Q7: How can I prevent dye migration from affecting the connection with other components?

    Reason: Dye migration can cause color transfer to adjacent plastics or packaging materials, affecting overall product quality.

    Solution:

    • Select low-migration dyes and test their stability in actual application environments.
    • Coat the product surface with a protective layer (such as clear lacquer) to isolate the dye from external contact.
    • Avoid direct contact between dyed parts and materials with high oil absorption (such as soft PVC).
  • Q8: Does lightfastness and migration resistance change due to processing conditions?

    Reason: Excessively high processing temperatures (e.g., PC exceeding 320°C) can damage the dye structure, affecting lightfastness; excessively low temperatures can lead to incomplete dissolution, increasing migration risk.

    Solution:

    • Adjust the processing temperature according to the dye's thermal tolerance range (e.g., anthraquinone dyes are suitable for 260-300°C).
    • Maintain stable screw speed and pressure to ensure the dye melts fully and disperses evenly.
    • Record lightfastness and migration resistance performance under different conditions during trial production to optimize processing parameters.
  • Q9: How can solubility be guaranteed while controlling costs?

    Reason: Dyes with high solubility (such as certain specific anthraquinones) are more costly, which can increase production expenses.

    Solution:

    • Select cost-effective dyes based on product requirements (e.g., Solvent Orange 60 is suitable for ABS).
    • Reduce the dye concentration and achieve the target color through color matching techniques (e.g., using a small amount of complementary color dye).
    • Negotiate with suppliers to bulk purchase specific dyes to reduce unit price.
  • Q10: How are the migration resistance, lightfastness, and solubility of solvent dyes tested?

    Reason: Without testing, it may not be possible to predict the dye's performance in actual applications, affecting product longevity.

    Solution:

    • Migration resistance test: Press the dyed sample against wet paper or white cloth under high-temperature conditions (60°C, 95%RH, 24 hours) and observe the color transfer.
    • Lightfastness test: Test the degree of fading using a xenon lamp according to the ISO 105-B02 standard.
    • Solubility test: Gradually increase the dye concentration in a small sample, observe whether there is precipitation or color streaks, and determine the concentration limit.
